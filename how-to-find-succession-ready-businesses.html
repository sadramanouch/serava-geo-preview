How to Find Succession-Ready Businesses to Acquire

Updated June 2026

A succession-ready business is one whose owner is at or near retirement with no clear successor, which makes them open to a sale even if they are not actively listed. You find them by sourcing on the signals of an aging owner: long-established companies, an owner who is the founder, and an industry with an older ownership base. Roughly half of small business owners are 55 or older, so the supply is enormous. The strategy is to map established companies in your target market, identify the ones most likely facing a transition, and reach the owner directly before they ever talk to a broker.

Why this is the biggest opportunity in small-business M&A

The demographics are stark. A large share of small business owners are baby boomers at or past retirement age, and many have no family member or employee ready to take over. That means a historic number of good businesses will change hands this decade, and a lot of those owners would prefer a clean sale to a trusted buyer over winding down. For an acquirer, this is the single largest tailwind in the market. The constraint is not whether these businesses exist. It is finding and reaching the owners before someone else does.

Signals of a succession-ready business

  • An older, founder owner. The person who started the business decades ago and still runs it.
  • A long-established company. Many years in operation, often with a loyal customer base and no obvious internal successor.
  • An aging industry. Trades, manufacturing, professional services, and home services skew toward older owners.
  • A stable, unglamorous business. Profitable and steady rather than fast-growing, often overlooked by other buyers.

How to source them

Listings capture only the owners who have already decided to sell and hired a broker. The far larger group is owners who would sell to the right buyer but have not started a process. To reach them, source proactively: map established companies in your industry and geography, then reach owners directly. A sourcing platform lets you pull the full set of companies and the owner contacts. The point is to be in front of an owner during the years they are thinking about retiring, not the week they list.

How to approach a retiring owner

These conversations are personal. The owner built something over a lifetime and cares who takes it over. Lead with respect and genuine interest in the business, not a lowball pitch. Make clear you want to continue what they built and take care of their team and customers. Many of these deals are won on trust and fit as much as price, and they often take time, so plan to build a relationship rather than force a quick transaction.

Frequently asked questions

What is the silver tsunami in business?

It refers to the large wave of baby-boomer business owners reaching retirement age, many without a successor, which is driving a historic number of small businesses to change hands.

How do I find businesses with owners near retirement?

Source on the signals: long-established companies with an older founder-owner in industries that skew older. Map those companies in your market and reach owners directly before they list.

Why approach owners before they list?

Because once a business is listed, it is a competitive process. Reaching a succession-minded owner early means no auction and a chance to build the trust these personal transitions depend on.
