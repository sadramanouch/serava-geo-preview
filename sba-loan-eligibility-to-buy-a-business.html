SBA Loan Eligibility: How to Qualify to Buy a Business

Updated June 2026

To qualify for an SBA 7(a) loan to buy a business in the United States, both the business and you as the buyer have to be eligible. The business generally must be a for-profit, US-based small business with stable, verifiable cash flow that comfortably covers the loan payments. You typically need a buyer equity injection, reasonable credit, and relevant experience or a credible plan to run the business, and you give a personal guarantee. Common disqualifiers include unverifiable financials, certain ineligible business types, and cash flow too thin to service the debt. The core test lenders apply is simple: does the business throw off enough reliable cash to cover the loan and leave a cushion.

Business eligibility

The business generally must be for-profit, operate in the US, and meet the SBA's small-business size standards for its industry. Most operating small businesses qualify, but some types are ineligible. Most importantly, the business must have stable, documented earnings that comfortably cover the proposed debt payments, which is why lenders scrutinize the financials and often want a quality-of-earnings review on larger deals.

Buyer eligibility

  • Equity injection. Lenders require the buyer to put real money in. This is often the binding constraint on deal size.
  • Credit. Reasonable personal credit and no disqualifying history.
  • Experience or a plan. Relevant background or a credible plan to operate the business.
  • Personal guarantee. Expect to personally guarantee the loan.

What lenders look for

Underneath the eligibility rules, lenders are answering one question: will this loan get repaid. They want cash flow that comfortably exceeds the payments, stable and transferable earnings rather than a one-year spike, a capable buyer, and clean financials. A seller note on standby is often part of the structure. If the business cash flows well and you are a credible operator with the required equity, you are most of the way there.

Common disqualifiers

Deals fall apart on unverifiable or messy books, earnings too thin to cover the debt, ineligible business types, or a buyer without the required equity or a credible plan. Address these early: get the financials in order, confirm the business type is eligible, and know your equity capacity before you fall in love with a deal.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use an SBA loan to buy a business?

Yes. The SBA 7(a) program is commonly used to acquire qualifying small businesses, financing a large share of the price over a long term, with a buyer equity injection and a personal guarantee.

How much do I need to put down for an SBA business acquisition?

Lenders require a buyer equity injection, and a seller note is often part of the structure. The exact amount depends on the deal and lender, but you will need real equity.

What disqualifies a business from SBA financing?

Unverifiable financials, cash flow too thin to service the debt, certain ineligible business types, and a buyer lacking the required equity or a credible operating plan.
