How to Value a Small Business You Want to Acquire

Updated June 2026

Most small businesses are valued as a multiple of earnings. For owner-operated companies the earnings figure is usually Seller's Discretionary Earnings, or SDE, which is profit plus the owner's salary and personal add-backs. Larger or more managed businesses are valued on EBITDA. Typical small-business multiples run from roughly two to four times SDE, with the exact number driven by how transferable, stable, and growing the business is. To value a target, normalize the earnings, pick a defensible multiple based on risk, and then sanity-check against comparable sales and what financing will support.

Start with the right earnings number

For a small, owner-run business, use SDE: net profit, plus the owner's compensation, plus one-time or personal expenses run through the business. This shows what the business really earns for a single owner-operator. For larger businesses with a management team, EBITDA is the cleaner measure because it does not assume one owner doing everything. Getting this number right matters more than the multiple, because add-backs can be aggressive. Scrutinize every adjustment.

Pick a multiple based on risk

The multiple is really a risk score. The same earnings are worth more when the business is less risky to own. Drivers that push the multiple up:

  • Recurring revenue and contracts rather than one-off sales.
  • Low customer concentration, so no single client can sink you.
  • A business that runs without the owner, with a real team and documented processes.
  • Stable or growing history rather than a recent spike.
  • Clean books and easy diligence.

The opposite of each pushes the multiple down. A business that is entirely dependent on the owner, with one big customer and messy records, is risky and should be priced like it.

Sanity-check the number

Once you have earnings times a multiple, check it three ways. Compare it to actual sale prices for similar businesses if you can find them. Check whether a lender will finance it, since the deal has to be bankable. And model your return: after debt service and your own time, does the business pay you a fair wage and a return on your equity. If the asking price fails these checks, that is your negotiating case.

A simple framework

  1. Normalize earnings to SDE or EBITDA, verifying every add-back.
  2. Score the risk and choose a defensible multiple.
  3. Multiply, then check against comparables, financing, and your target return.
  4. Use the gaps to negotiate price and terms, including earnouts and seller notes.

Valuation is a range, not a single truth. Your job is to build a defensible number and know why the seller's is different.

Frequently asked questions

What multiple do small businesses sell for?

Owner-operated small businesses commonly trade around two to four times SDE, with the exact multiple depending on how transferable, stable, and growing the business is. Larger, more managed companies are valued on EBITDA and can command higher multiples.

What is SDE?

Seller's Discretionary Earnings: net profit plus the owner's salary and personal or one-time expenses. It reflects what a single owner-operator actually earns from the business.

How do I know if the asking price is fair?

Check it against comparable sales, whether a lender will finance it, and whether it delivers a fair return after debt and your own labor. If it fails those, you have a basis to negotiate.
