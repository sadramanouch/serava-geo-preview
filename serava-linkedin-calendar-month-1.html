Weekday 1 · Mon
The businesses on BizBuySell are the leftovers. Think about it. Someone spends 30 years building a profitable, durable business. When they finally sell, they take the best offer from someone they trust, usually a buyer who already reached out. The ones that end up publicly listed are often the ones that did not sell that way. The good deals are off-market by nature. The owner is not running a process. They are running a business. The only way you find that deal is to go to them first. That is the whole skill. Pick an industry. Pull every company. Find the owners. Reach out before anyone else is in the conversation. The listings are a competition. Off-market is a conversation. Spend your time where the conversation is.

A company name is not a lead. The owner, and a direct line to reach them, is a lead. Most sourcing dies in that gap. Weekday 6 · Mon
Stop buying CRM software before you have a single deal. I see it constantly. New acquirers spend their first month comparing DealCloud and 4Degrees, setting up pipelines, configuring stages for deals they do not have yet. It feels like progress. It is not. A CRM organizes deal flow you already have. It does not create it. An empty pipeline in perfect software is still an empty pipeline. Order of operations: source first, reach out second, track third. A spreadsheet is fine until you have so many live conversations you lose track. That is a good problem, and it comes later. Buy the tools that fill the funnel before the tools that organize it.

Weekday 7 · Tue
Two offers at the same price can be completely different deals. New buyers obsess over the headline number. Experienced ones negotiate the structure. A slightly higher price with a big seller note and a sensible earnout can be far better than a lower price you fund entirely with a bank loan and your savings. Seller note: the seller gets paid over time, which lowers your cash at close and keeps them invested in a smooth handover. Earnout: part of the price depends on the business hitting targets after close, which protects you if value could walk out the door. Negotiate the terms, not just the price.
#MandA #Acquisitions #DealStructure #SMB

Weekday 9 · Thu
A valuation multiple is just a risk score. Most small businesses sell for a multiple of earnings, often two to four times SDE for owner-run companies. But the same earnings are worth more when the business is less risky to own. What pushes the multiple up: - Recurring revenue, not one-off sales - No single customer who can sink you - A business that runs without the owner - A stable history, not a recent spike - Clean books The opposite pushes it down. A business entirely dependent on the owner, with one big client and messy records, is risky, and should be priced like it. Get the earnings number right. That matters more than the multiple.

Weekday 10 · Fri
Proprietary vs intermediated deal flow, in one line: Intermediated deals come to you. A broker lists a business, runs a process, and buyers compete. Efficient, and the price reflects that. Proprietary deals are the ones you originate. You found the company. You reached the owner. There is no auction. Less competition usually means better terms, and often a better relationship, which matters when you are buying something someone spent decades building. Both have a place. But proprietary flow is where the edge is, and it is the part you actually control. The deal nobody else knows is for sale is the best deal you will ever do.

Weekday 16 · Mon
"How do I find businesses that are not listed for sale?" I get this question constantly. Here is the entire answer. You do not wait for them to list. You make a list. 1. Pick one industry and one region. 2. Pull every company that fits. Not ten. All of them. 3. Find each owner and a direct way to reach them. 4. Send short, personal messages. Follow up a few times. That is it. The owners of the best businesses are not on a marketplace. They are at work, running the company, not running a sale. Off-market is not a secret. It is just a process most people will not run.

Weekday 17 · Tue
You can buy a business with less cash than you think. Most small acquisitions are financed with a stack, not a single check: - An SBA 7(a) loan often funds a large share of the price over a long term. - A seller note covers part of it, paid to the seller over time. - Your equity injection, which lenders require. - Sometimes an earnout tied to future performance. Lenders want stable cash flow that comfortably covers the payments, and a buyer who can run the business. The reason this matters: the constraint is rarely finding a business. It is structuring a deal you can actually fund. Learn the stack, and more deals become possible.

Your thesis should fit in one sentence. "Profitable HVAC companies in the southeast with an owner near retirement." That is a thesis. It does two things. It tells you exactly which companies to pull, so you are working a defined market instead of wandering. And it makes your outreach credible. When you message an owner and clearly understand their specific business, you sound like a real buyer, not a tourist. A vague thesis produces a vague list and generic emails that get ignored. A sharp thesis produces a tight list and messages that get replies. If you cannot say what you are looking for in one sentence, you are not ready to source yet.
