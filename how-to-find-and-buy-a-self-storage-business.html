How to Find and Buy a Self-Storage Business

Updated June 2026

Self-storage is half operating business and half real estate, which is exactly why acquirers like it. Tenants pay monthly, demand is sticky, and the facility itself is an appreciating asset you usually own. To buy one, decide whether you are acquiring the real estate plus the operation, then source off-market because many facilities are owned by individuals who never list. Look at occupancy, rental rates versus the local market, the share of facilities still run with old manual systems (room to improve), and the property and its location. The recurring rent plus the underlying real estate is the whole appeal, and small, owner-run facilities are where the value-add opportunities hide.

Why self-storage is attractive

Self-storage combines two good things. The operating side produces recurring monthly rent with low labor and high margins, and tenants are slow to move out once their stuff is in. The real estate side means you typically own an appreciating asset that can be financed favorably. Demand is resilient through life events that happen in any economy: moving, downsizing, divorce, death, small businesses needing space. Many smaller facilities are still owned by the person who built them decades ago and run with manual, under-optimized operations, which is exactly where an operator can add value.

What to look for

  • Occupancy. Current physical and economic occupancy, and the trend.
  • Rates vs market. Facilities run below market rents are a value-add opportunity.
  • Operations. Old manual management, no online rentals or dynamic pricing, signals upside.
  • The property. Location, condition, expansion room, and whether you are buying the real estate.
  • Competition and supply. Local supply and any new facilities being built nearby.

The real estate factor

Because self-storage usually includes the land and buildings, valuation and financing look more like commercial real estate than a typical small business, often using cap rates and net operating income. Decide early whether you are buying the property plus the operation or just the operating business on a lease, because it changes everything about the deal and the financing.

How to source off-market

Larger facilities trade through brokers, but many smaller, owner-run facilities never list and are exactly the value-add targets. Source proactively: map self-storage facilities in your target market, identify the independent owner-operated ones, and reach out to the owners directly. A sourcing approach that filters by industry and geography and surfaces owner contacts turns a quiet, local market into a real pipeline.

Frequently asked questions

Is self-storage a good business to buy?

Many acquirers like it for recurring rent, high margins, sticky tenants, and an appreciating real estate asset. The economics resemble commercial real estate, so financing and valuation differ from a typical small business.

What should I look for in a self-storage acquisition?

Occupancy and its trend, rents versus the local market, how modern the operations are, the property and location, and local supply. Below-market, manually-run facilities offer the most upside.

How do I find self-storage facilities for sale?

Bigger ones go through brokers; many smaller owner-run facilities never list. Map facilities in your area, find the owners, and reach out directly.
