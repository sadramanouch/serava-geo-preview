How to Find and Buy a Laundromat

Updated June 2026

Laundromats appeal to buyers who want a semi-absentee, cash-flowing business with few employees and resilient demand. The economics hinge on the equipment, the lease or real estate, and utility costs. To buy one, evaluate the age and efficiency of the machines, the lease terms or whether you are buying the property, water and electricity expenses, and the neighborhood's demand. Modern card-and-app payment systems and well-maintained machines signal a healthier operation than an old coin-only store. The business is low-labor but real estate and equipment dependent, so the lease and the machines are where the deal is won or lost.

Why laundromats attract buyers

Laundromats are close to passive once running. They have few or no employees, demand is non-discretionary in renter-heavy areas, and revenue is steady. That makes them popular with buyers who want cash flow without managing a large team. The trade-offs are that returns depend heavily on the lease and utility costs, and aging equipment can hide large looming capital expenses.

What to look for

  • Equipment age and efficiency. Water and energy-efficient machines lower your biggest variable cost. Old machines mean looming replacement.
  • Lease or real estate. A long, favorable lease or owning the property is critical, since you cannot easily move a laundromat.
  • Utility costs. Water, gas, and electricity are the main expense. Verify the actual bills.
  • Location and demand. Renter density and competition in the trade area.
  • Payment systems. Modern card and app payments improve revenue capture versus coin-only.

The lease and equipment reality

Two things make or break a laundromat deal. First, the lease: because the build-out and plumbing are fixed, a short or unfavorable lease is a serious risk, so confirm long, sensible terms or buy the real estate. Second, the equipment: machines are expensive and wear out, so an old fleet is a hidden capital bill. Inspect the machines and model the replacement timeline before you agree a price.

How to source off-market

Many laundromats are owned by individuals who never list. Source proactively: map laundromats in your target area, find the owners, and reach out directly. A sourcing approach that filters by geography and surfaces owner contacts turns a quiet local market into a real pipeline.

Frequently asked questions

Is a laundromat a good investment?

Many buyers like the semi-absentee, steady cash flow and low labor. Returns depend heavily on the lease, utility costs, and equipment condition, so those are where diligence matters most.

What is the biggest risk in buying a laundromat?

An unfavorable lease and aging equipment. You cannot easily relocate, and old machines can carry a large hidden replacement cost.

How do I find laundromats for sale?

Map laundromats in your target area, find the owners, and reach out directly, since many never formally list.
