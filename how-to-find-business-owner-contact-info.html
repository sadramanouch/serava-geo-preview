How to Find a Business Owner's Contact Information for an Acquisition

Updated June 2026

To reach a business owner about acquiring their company, you need three things: the owner's name, a direct email, and a phone number. You can assemble these from public records, the company website and LinkedIn, state business registries, and contact-enrichment tools. For one or two companies, do it by hand. For a list of dozens or hundreds, use a sourcing platform that enriches owner contacts automatically, because manual lookup does not scale and a generic info address will not get you a reply.

Start with the name

You cannot personalize outreach without knowing who owns or runs the business. Check the company website's about and team pages, LinkedIn, and state business registries, which often list a registered agent or officer. For smaller owner-operated companies, the person listed on the filing is frequently the decision maker you want.

Then get a direct email and phone

A generic info or contact address is the weakest possible channel. You want the owner's direct line. Sources that help:

  • The company website, sometimes a direct email is published for the owner or principal.
  • LinkedIn, to confirm the role and sometimes a contact path.
  • State and county business filings, for registered names and addresses.
  • Contact-enrichment tools, which match a person at a company to a likely direct email and phone.

Always verify an email before you send to it. Verified addresses get far better reply rates and protect your sending domain from bounces and spam flags.

The manual method versus a tool

Finding one owner by hand is easy. Finding two hundred is a part-time job. If your acquisition strategy depends on contacting a full market of companies, manual lookup becomes the bottleneck. This is the main reason searchers turn to sourcing platforms that already pair each company with owner contact details. Grata and Sourcescrub serve funded teams. Do it responsibly

Cold outreach to business owners is legitimate, but it comes with rules. Follow email regulations such as CAN-SPAM in the United States and CASL in Canada, honor opt-outs, keep your messages truthful, and respect do-not-contact requests. Responsible outreach is also better outreach, because it protects your reputation and your sending domain.

Turn contacts into conversations

Good contact data is only step one. The reply comes from a short, specific, human message that references the actual business and makes a low-pressure ask, followed up a few times. Owners ignore generic blasts and respond to messages that sound like a real person who did their homework.

Frequently asked questions

How do I find the owner of a company I want to buy?

Start with the company website, LinkedIn, and state business registries to get the name, then use enrichment tools or filings to find a direct email and phone. For a single target this is quick. For a full list, use a sourcing tool that attaches owner contacts.

Is it legal to cold email business owners about buying their company?

Yes, business-to-business cold outreach is legal in most places when you follow the rules, such as CAN-SPAM in the US and CASL in Canada, identify yourself honestly, and honor opt-outs.

What is the fastest way to get owner contacts for a whole market?

A sourcing platform that filters companies by industry and geography and enriches them with owner contact details. That turns a research task into an export. Serava is built for this on a searcher budget.

Why not just use the info@ email?

Generic inboxes are screened or ignored and rarely reach the owner. A verified direct email to the decision maker gets far higher reply rates.
