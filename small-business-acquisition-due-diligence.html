Small Business Acquisition Due Diligence: A Practical Checklist

Updated June 2026

Due diligence is where you confirm the business is what the seller says it is, before money changes hands. For a small business, focus on four areas: financial (verify the earnings and the add-backs), customer (concentration and retention), operational (how dependent is it on the owner, and will the staff stay), and legal (contracts, licenses, liabilities). The red flags that kill deals are messy or unverifiable books, one customer who is most of the revenue, a business that cannot run without the owner, and undisclosed legal or tax problems. Bring in an accountant for a quality-of-earnings review and a lawyer for the contracts. You can run the rest yourself.

1. Financial diligence

This is the core. Verify the earnings the price is based on. Tie the reported numbers to tax returns and bank statements, not just a spreadsheet. Scrutinize every add-back, since SDE adjustments can be aggressive. For anything beyond a tiny deal, get a quality-of-earnings review from an accountant. You are confirming the business actually earns what the seller claims.

2. Customer diligence

  • Concentration. If one or two customers are most of the revenue, the business is fragile. Know the top-customer share.
  • Retention. Are customers long-tenured and recurring, or churning and one-off?
  • Relationships. Are they tied to the company, or to the owner who is leaving?

3. Operational diligence

  • Owner dependence. The biggest small-business risk. If the owner is the top salesperson, only estimator, and sole relationship holder, plan for that.
  • Staff. Will key people stay through the transition? The team is often most of the value.
  • Systems. Documented processes and modern software make the handover far smoother.

4. Legal and tax diligence

Have a lawyer review the contracts, leases, and any licenses or permits, and confirm there are no undisclosed liabilities, lawsuits, or tax issues. Make sure key contracts and licenses transfer with the sale. This is not where you cut corners.

Red flags that should slow you down

  • Books that do not tie to tax returns, or a seller reluctant to share them.
  • One customer who is a large share of revenue.
  • A business completely dependent on the departing owner.
  • Undisclosed legal, tax, or licensing problems.
  • Key staff who will not commit to staying.

Frequently asked questions

What is the most important part of small business due diligence?

Financial diligence: verifying the earnings the price is based on against tax returns and bank statements, and scrutinizing the add-backs. A quality-of-earnings review is worth it.

What are the biggest red flags when buying a business?

Unverifiable books, high customer concentration, total dependence on the departing owner, undisclosed legal or tax issues, and key staff who will not stay.

Do I need professionals for diligence?

Yes for the financial and legal parts: an accountant for a quality-of-earnings review and a lawyer for contracts and liabilities. You can handle much of the operational and customer review yourself.
