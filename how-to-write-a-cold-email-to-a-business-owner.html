How to Write a Cold Email to a Business Owner About Buying Their Company

Updated June 2026

The cold emails that owners answer are short, specific, respectful, and human. Keep it under 120 words. Make it obvious you researched their specific business, say plainly that you are interested in acquiring it, and make a low-pressure ask for a conversation, not a price. Skip the corporate jargon and the hard sell. Then follow up two or three times over a few weeks, because most replies come on a later touch. Reply rates are single digits even when you do this well, so personalization and persistence across a large list are what produce conversations.

The structure that works

  1. A specific opener. Reference their actual business. "I came across [Company] and was impressed by [specific thing]." This alone separates you from the spam.
  2. Why them. One line on why you are reaching out to this business in particular. Owners can tell generic from genuine instantly.
  3. The plain ask. Say you are interested in acquiring a business like theirs, and ask if they would be open to a short, no-pressure conversation. Do not lead with price or terms.
  4. An easy out. "Totally understand if the timing is not right." Lowering the stakes raises the reply rate.

Tone and what to avoid

  • Be a person, not a fund. Owners respond to humans, not corporate boilerplate.
  • Keep it short. A long email reads like a pitch. A short one reads like a real note.
  • No lowball, no pressure. You are starting a relationship, not opening a negotiation.
  • Respect what they built. Many owners care more about who takes over than about squeezing the last dollar.

A simple template

Subject: a question about [Company]

Hi [First name],

I came across [Company] and was genuinely impressed by [specific detail]. I am looking to acquire and operate a business like yours in [industry/area], and I wanted to reach out directly.

Would you be open to a short, no-pressure call in the next couple of weeks? Totally understand if the timing is not right.

Thanks for considering it,
[Your name] · [phone]

The follow-up is where deals happen

Most owners who eventually reply do so on the second or third message, not the first. They were busy, or not thinking about it that week. A short, polite follow-up every week or two, two or three times, reads as serious, not pushy. Send the first email, then plan the follow-ups before you move on.

Frequently asked questions

How long should an acquisition cold email be?

Short. Under about 120 words. A long email reads like a pitch; a short, specific one reads like a real person reaching out.

Should I mention price in the first email?

No. Lead with genuine interest and a low-pressure ask for a conversation. Price comes much later, after a relationship and real information.

How many follow-ups should I send?

Two or three, spaced a week or two apart. Most replies come on a later touch, so the follow-up is often where the conversation actually starts.
