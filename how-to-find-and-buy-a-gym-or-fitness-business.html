How to Find and Buy a Gym or Fitness Business

Updated June 2026

Gyms and fitness studios run on memberships, which makes them recurring-revenue businesses, but the model lives or dies on churn. To buy one, focus on the active membership base, the monthly churn rate, the mix of membership versus personal training and class revenue, and the lease. A studio with loyal members and low churn is valuable; one that signs members fast but loses them just as fast is a treadmill. Understand the format, since a boutique studio, a 24-hour access gym, and a full-service club have very different economics. The retained membership base and the lease are what you are really buying.

Why fitness businesses can be attractive

Memberships create recurring monthly revenue, and a well-run studio with a loyal community can be steady and profitable. Personal training, classes, and retail add higher-margin revenue on top. The category is large and fragmented, with many independent owners. The risk is that fitness has high member churn and is competitive, so a gym's value depends entirely on how well it retains members, not how many it signs.

What to look for

  • Active members and churn. The retained, paying base and the monthly cancellation rate are the core metrics.
  • Revenue mix. Memberships plus training, classes, and retail. Diversified is stronger.
  • The lease. A fitness build-out is fixed and costly, so lease terms are critical.
  • Format and competition. Boutique, access gym, or full-service, and who else is nearby.
  • Owner and trainer dependence. Whether members are loyal to the gym or to a specific trainer.

The churn reality

Fitness is notorious for churn. Members sign up with enthusiasm and cancel within months, so a gym that adds members quickly can still shrink if retention is poor. Scrutinize the churn rate and the trend, understand what keeps members engaged, and value the business on retained, recurring membership revenue rather than gross sign-ups.

How to source off-market

Many independent gyms and studios are owner-run and never list. Source proactively: map fitness businesses in your target market, find the owners, and reach out directly. A sourcing platform that filters by industry and geography and surfaces owner contacts turns a fragmented, competitive market into a workable target list.

Frequently asked questions

Is buying a gym a good investment?

It can be, when the gym has a loyal membership base and low churn, plus training and class revenue. The risk is high churn and competition, so retention is everything.

What is the most important metric for a gym acquisition?

Active members and the churn rate. A gym is only as valuable as the membership revenue it retains, not the number it signs.

How do I find gyms for sale?

Map fitness businesses in your area, find the owners, and reach out directly, since many independents never list.
