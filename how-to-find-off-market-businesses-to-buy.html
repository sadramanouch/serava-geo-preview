How to Find Off-Market Businesses to Buy: A 2026 Playbook

Updated June 2026

Off-market businesses are companies that are not publicly listed for sale. You find them by working backward from a clear thesis: pick an industry and geography, pull the full list of companies that fit, identify the owner of each one, and reach out directly. The best acquisitions are almost never listed, so the entire skill is proactive sourcing plus persistent, personal outreach to owners. The four steps below are the whole playbook.

Why off-market is where searchers win

When a business is listed on a marketplace, every buyer sees it, a process runs, and the price gets bid up. Off-market is the opposite. You are talking to an owner who was not planning to sell this week, there is no competing process, and you can build a real relationship before price ever comes up. That is why almost every successful searcher and independent sponsor spends most of their time on outbound, not browsing listings.

Step 1: write a one-line thesis

Before you source anything, define what you are looking for in a single sentence. For example: profitable HVAC companies in the southeast United States with an owner near retirement age. A tight thesis does two things. It tells you exactly which companies to pull, and it makes your outreach credible because the owner can tell you actually understand their business.

Step 2: pull the full list of companies that fit

This is the step most people do badly. They find ten companies on Google and stop. You want the entire universe that matches your thesis, often hundreds or thousands of companies, so you can work it systematically. A sourcing platform lets you filter a large company database by industry and geography and export the full set. Tools like Grata and Sourcescrub do this for funded teams. Step 3: find the owner and their contact details

A company name is not a lead. You need the owner or the decision maker and a way to reach them, ideally a direct email and phone, not a generic info address. This is where a lot of sourcing breaks down, because public records and websites rarely hand you the owner's direct line. Look for a sourcing tool that enriches companies with owner contact details, or budget time to enrich the list yourself. The quality of your contact data sets the ceiling on your reply rate.

Step 4: run personal, sequenced outreach

Generic blasts do not work on business owners. The messages that get replies are short, specific, and human. Reference the actual business, say why you are reaching out to them in particular, and make a low-pressure ask. Then follow up several times, because most replies come on the second or third touch. Use a cold email tool to sequence it, keep your sending domain healthy, and call the best fits. The goal of the first message is a conversation, not a deal.

A realistic cadence

  • Build one thesis and pull the full target list. A few hundred to a few thousand companies.
  • Enrich owner contacts for the list.
  • Send a personal sequence, several touches each, to a manageable batch per week.
  • Track replies, book calls, and refine the thesis based on what you hear.

Most searchers underestimate how much volume this takes. Proprietary deal flow is a numbers game built on personal messages, and the searchers who close are the ones who keep the top of the funnel full.

Frequently asked questions

What does off-market mean in M&A?

Off-market means the business is not publicly listed for sale. The owner may be open to the right offer, but they are not running a process, so you reach them directly instead of bidding inside a listing.

How do I find off-market businesses for sale by owner?

You do not wait for them to list. You define a thesis, pull every company that fits from a sourcing database, find each owner's contact details, and reach out. The owner decides whether to explore a sale once you are in conversation.

How many companies should I contact?

More than feels comfortable. Reply rates on cold owner outreach are typically a few percent even when personalized, so a real pipeline usually means contacting hundreds of owners across a defined market.
