Seller Financing Explained for Business Acquisitions

Updated June 2026

Seller financing is when the seller agrees to be paid part of the purchase price over time instead of all at closing, effectively lending you a portion of the deal. It is one of the most useful tools in small-business acquisitions. For you, it lowers the cash you need up front and can fill the gap between a bank loan and your equity. For the seller, it can mean a higher total price, spread out tax, and a smoother transition. It also keeps the seller invested in your success, since they only get fully paid if the business keeps performing. Many SBA-backed deals include a seller note as part of the structure.

How a seller note works

Instead of receiving the entire price at closing, the seller takes a promissory note for part of it, which you repay over a set term with interest. The terms, the amount, interest rate, length, and whether payments are on standby for a period, are negotiated as part of the deal. The result is that you fund less cash up front, and the seller becomes, in effect, one of your lenders.

Why it benefits both sides

  • For you: less cash at closing, a filled financing gap, and a seller motivated to hand off the business well.
  • For the seller: often a higher total price, interest income, spread-out taxes, and confidence the business is in good hands.
  • For the deal: a seller willing to finance part of the price is also signaling confidence in the business, which is reassuring.

Where it fits in the stack

Seller financing usually sits alongside other sources. A common small-business structure combines a bank or SBA loan for the bulk of the price, a seller note for a portion, and your equity injection. The seller note reduces how much you need from the bank and from your own pocket, and in many SBA deals a seller note on standby is part of qualifying. Used well, seller financing makes more deals possible and better structured.

Frequently asked questions

What is seller financing?

An arrangement where the seller is paid part of the purchase price over time rather than all at closing, effectively lending you a portion of the deal through a seller note.

Why would a seller finance the sale?

To get a higher total price, earn interest, spread out taxes, and ensure a smooth transition. It also signals their confidence in the business.

Is seller financing common when buying a small business?

Very. It is one of the most common tools in small-business acquisitions and is often part of the structure in SBA-backed deals.
