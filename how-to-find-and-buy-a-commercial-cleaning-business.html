How to Find and Buy a Commercial Cleaning or Janitorial Business

Updated June 2026

Commercial cleaning is a recurring-contract business: offices, medical buildings, and facilities pay monthly for ongoing janitorial service, and those contracts renew. That predictable, sticky revenue is the appeal. To buy one, decide on a contract size and customer type, then source off-market because the market is fragmented and most owners never list. Look for a high share of recurring contract revenue, low customer concentration, and a stable crew, and understand that labor is the central challenge in this trade: turnover is high and the team executes the contracts. The recurring book is the asset. One-off jobs are not what you are buying.

Why commercial cleaning is attractive

Cleaning is non-discretionary for most businesses, and it is contracted, so revenue is recurring and predictable. Margins are healthy, startup capital is low, and the industry is enormous and fragmented, full of owner-operators who built a local book of contracts and are aging toward retirement. That mix of sticky recurring revenue and a fragmented, retiring owner base makes it a steady hunting ground for searchers and home and facility-services consolidators.

What to look for

  • Recurring contract share. Monthly janitorial contracts are the value. One-time cleans are not.
  • Customer concentration. One building that is most of revenue is a real risk.
  • Contract terms and retention. Length, renewal, and how long clients have stayed.
  • Crew stability. Labor turnover is the industry's defining challenge. A reliable team is worth a lot.
  • Margins and pricing. Below-market pricing can be an upside; thin margins can be a trap.

The labor reality

Cleaning is labor-intensive and turnover runs high. The business is essentially managing crews to reliably execute recurring contracts. Before you buy, understand how the company recruits, schedules, and retains workers, and how dependent the operation is on the owner personally covering shifts or relationships. Strong labor systems are a genuine competitive advantage here.

How to source off-market

A single metro holds many independent cleaning companies, most of which never list. Source proactively: map every commercial cleaning and janitorial company in your target area, find the owners, and reach out directly. A sourcing platform that filters by industry and geography and surfaces owner contacts turns a fragmented market into a workable target list.

Frequently asked questions

Is a commercial cleaning business a good acquisition?

Yes for many buyers: recurring contracts, non-discretionary demand, low capital needs, and a fragmented, retiring owner base. The main challenge is labor turnover.

What makes a cleaning business valuable?

The share of recurring monthly contracts, customer retention and diversification, and a stable crew. One-time job revenue is worth far less.

How do I find cleaning businesses to buy?

Map every operator in your target area, find the owners, and reach out directly, since the market is fragmented and most never formally list.
