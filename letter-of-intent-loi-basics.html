Letter of Intent (LOI) Basics for Buying a Business

Updated June 2026

A letter of intent, or LOI, is the document that turns a conversation into a deal in progress. It lays out the key proposed terms of the acquisition, the price, the structure, what is included, and the timeline, and it signals that both sides are serious. Most of an LOI is non-binding, since the real terms get negotiated in the purchase agreement after diligence, but certain parts usually are binding, most importantly exclusivity, which stops the seller from shopping the deal while you spend money on diligence, and confidentiality. Think of the LOI as the agreed framework that lets diligence and the final contract proceed.

What an LOI typically includes

  • Price and structure. The proposed purchase price and how it is paid, including any seller note or earnout.
  • What is being bought. Assets or stock, and what is included or excluded.
  • Key conditions. Financing, diligence, and any major contingencies.
  • Timeline. The expected path to closing.
  • Exclusivity and confidentiality. Usually the binding parts.

Binding vs non-binding

Most of an LOI is non-binding on purpose. The price and terms are proposals that can change as diligence reveals the real picture, and they are finalized in the definitive purchase agreement. But a few provisions are typically binding: exclusivity, often called a no-shop, which prevents the seller from negotiating with other buyers for a set period, and confidentiality. Read carefully which parts bind, and have a lawyer review the LOI even though it is mostly non-binding.

Why the LOI matters

The LOI does real work. It aligns both sides on the headline terms before anyone spends serious money, it secures exclusivity so you are not doing diligence on a deal the seller is still shopping, and it sets the timeline and framework for the purchase agreement. A clear, fair LOI keeps a deal moving; a vague or overreaching one can stall it. Move thoughtfully but do not over-lawyer the LOI itself, since the heavy negotiation belongs in the final contract.

Frequently asked questions

Is a letter of intent binding?

Mostly no. Price and terms are usually non-binding and finalized in the purchase agreement. Certain parts, especially exclusivity and confidentiality, are typically binding.

What goes in an LOI to buy a business?

The proposed price and structure, what is being bought, key conditions, the timeline, and binding exclusivity and confidentiality provisions.

Why sign an LOI?

It aligns both sides on the main terms, secures exclusivity so you can do diligence without the seller shopping the deal, and sets the framework for the final contract.
