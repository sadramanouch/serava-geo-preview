How Acquisition Financing Works: SBA Loans, Seller Notes, and Earnouts

Updated June 2026

Most small business acquisitions are financed with a stack, not a single source. In the United States, the backbone is often an SBA 7(a) loan, which can fund a large share of the purchase price for a qualifying business. The rest comes from a seller note, where the seller finances part of the price and gets paid over time, your own equity injection, and sometimes an earnout tied to future performance. Lenders want to see a business with stable cash flow that comfortably covers the debt payments, plus a buyer who can run it. The structure matters as much as the price, because terms determine your monthly payment and your risk.

The pieces of the stack

SBA 7(a) loan

For acquisitions of qualifying small businesses, an SBA 7(a) loan is the common backbone in the US. It can finance a large portion of the purchase price over a long term, which keeps payments manageable. In return, expect a personal guarantee, a required equity injection from the buyer, and real underwriting of both the business and you. The business must generate enough cash flow to cover the loan comfortably.

Seller financing

A seller note is when the seller agrees to be paid part of the price over time rather than all at closing. It is one of the most useful tools in a small deal. It reduces the cash you need up front, and it keeps the seller invested in a smooth handover. Many SBA deals require a seller note on standby as part of the structure.

Buyer equity

You put in real money. Lenders require it, and it aligns you with the outcome. This is often the constraint that determines what size of business you can buy.

Earnouts

An earnout ties part of the price to the business hitting agreed targets after closing. It bridges a gap when buyer and seller disagree on value, and it protects you if performance depends on things that could walk away, like a key client or the owner's relationships.

What lenders look for

  • Debt service coverage. Cash flow that comfortably exceeds the loan payments.
  • Stable, transferable earnings. Not a one-year spike, not all dependent on the seller.
  • A capable buyer. Relevant experience and a credible plan to run the business.
  • Clean financials. Diligence is far smoother when the books are in order.

Why structure beats price

A slightly higher price with a big seller note and a sensible earnout can be a far better deal than a lower price you have to fund entirely with a bank loan and your savings. Two offers at the same headline number can carry completely different risk. When you negotiate, negotiate the terms, not just the price.

Frequently asked questions

Can you buy a business with little money down?

Sometimes, by combining an SBA loan with a seller note and a modest equity injection. Lenders still require some buyer equity, but the stack can reduce how much cash you need at closing.

What is an SBA 7(a) loan used for?

Among other things, financing the acquisition of a qualifying small business. It can fund a large share of the price over a long term, with a personal guarantee and a buyer equity requirement.

What is a seller note?

Financing provided by the seller: they get paid part of the purchase price over time instead of all at closing, which lowers your upfront cash and keeps them invested in the transition.
